The one rule that unlocks it all

The Bromptons travel as luggage

Folded and bagged, a Brompton boards every train and ferry as free hand luggage -- no bike reservation, no fee, no refusal. That single move is what makes the whole rail plan work.

The handling routine

  • Fold and cover, every time. The cover is what turns "a bike" into "carry-on." It's effectively mandatory on the Polish Pendolinos and the safe default everywhere else.
  • Detach the Vincita rear bags before folding. The fold is cleaner and lighter as two pieces; the front carrier-block bag is your grab-and-go.
  • Target step-free stations. Loaded and front-heavy, the folded bike is a lift onto high luggage racks and over ferry stairs.
  • Don't ride the old-town cobbles on 16-inch wheels -- walk and fold there. Save the riding for the waterfront and river paths.

The three legs that aren't trains

  • Helsinki → Tallinn ferry. Book the cheap foot-passenger fare and carry the folded bikes on free -- never the vehicle/bicycle ticket.
  • Riga → Vilnius coach (~4h) and Vilnius → Gdańsk coach (~10½h, only ~2/day). Reserve the free bicycle ticket as insurance, and book the Gdańsk run early.
  • Never roll an unfolded bike onto a Polish EIP platform expecting to pay on board -- they don't sell bike tickets onboard. Folded and bagged sidesteps all of it.

Tickets & passes

One pass, a few reservations

The pass covers the trains; a handful of high-speed legs need a paid seat reservation on top. Bikes cost nothing, anywhere.

Eurail Global Pass · 2-month continuous · 2nd class

About €826 per person (~€1,652 the pair), mobile. The 15-day flexi (~€553) is too small once you count ten intercity legs plus all the covered day trips. Second class -- the free-bike rule is identical in both, so there's no upside to first.

Worth riding in daylight

Six trains to savour

Not just connections -- these are the journey. Sit by the window and time them for the light.

Tallinn → Tartu → Riga

The new Baltic line

Direct since January 2026, south through Estonian forest and bog. Break it at Tartu for the science centre.

Vienna → Ljubljana

The Semmering

The UNESCO mountain railway. Check your train runs the original viaduct line, not the new base tunnel -- if it tunnels, ride the historic stretch on a regional REX.

Day trip from Ljubljana

The Bohinj Railway

1906, turning 120 this year -- down the Bača and Soča valleys to Nova Gorica. Heritage steam on select summer dates.

Ljubljana → Villach → Salzburg

The Tauern Alps

The upper Sava, the Karawanken tunnel, then high Alpine scenery. Sit on the right leaving Villach.

Day trip from Munich

The Werdenfelsbahn

Up to Garmisch and the Zugspitze for the Bavarian Alps.

Mainz → Koblenz → Cologne

The West Rhine

The castles, vineyards and the Loreley. Force the route "via Koblenz" -- the default fast train takes the inland tunnel and misses the river entirely.

Before you go

Good to know

The practical layer -- money, plugs, data -- plus the handful of things this plan depends on.

Buy early
Lock the Eurail passes before you firm up dates.

Eurail often runs an early-summer promo (recently around 10-15%) -- check eurail.com for the current offer. The pass guarantees travel; the few required reservations are booked separately.

Five checks

  • Confirm NUK's Reading Room hours in Ljubljana -- it's only reliably open in the university summer break or on a guided tour. The one stop that can disappoint.
  • Check the Vienna → Ljubljana service runs the original Semmering viaduct, not the base tunnel -- or plan a regional leg over the old line.
  • Book the Vilnius → Gdańsk coach ahead and take an early departure, so the long day still ends in Gdańsk that evening.
  • Pre-book the two required Polish reservations (Gdańsk → Warsaw, Warsaw → Kraków) the moment dates are set.
  • Hold the two spare nights. The plan lands Aug 21 -- keep the buffer against a missed connection on the long German return, or spend it east.

Practicalities

  • Money. Euro everywhere except Poland (złoty). Cards work nearly everywhere; carry a little cash for kiosks and station lockers.
  • Plugs. Type C/F, 230V -- one EU adapter covers the whole trip; most chargers are already dual-voltage.
  • Data. One Europe-wide eSIM (Airalo / Nomad / Ubigi) roams across every country on the route -- simpler than swapping SIMs.
  • Water & tips. Tap water is safe throughout. Tipping is light -- round up, or 5-10% for good restaurant service.
  • Closures. Sunday retail is shut in Germany and Austria (limited in Poland); many museums close Mondays; expect some August holiday gaps.
  • Heat & lockers. Late July can hit 30-35°C, so ride early; big stations have left-luggage lockers (check one fits a folded Brompton).
